Automating Follow-Ups Without Being Annoying

Tanuj Muley · April 2025 · 5 min read

Back to blog

Automated follow-ups have a reputation problem. Most people have been on the receiving end of one — a generic "Just checking in!" sent at 9 AM on a Tuesday, clearly from a CRM, clearly written by no one. They feel like spam even when they're not.

The irony is that follow-ups done well are genuinely valuable. Most business relationships don't die from too many touchpoints — they die from the wrong ones, at the wrong time, with the wrong message. The goal of this post is to show you how to automate the volume without losing the quality.

Why most automated follow-ups fail

There are three main failure modes:

  • Generic openers. "Hope this email finds you well." It doesn't. And everyone knows it was generated.
  • No personalisation signal. The email could have been sent to anyone. If the recipient can tell it's a template, you've already lost.
  • Wrong timing. Following up two days after a proposal is different from following up two weeks after a call that went cold. The sequence matters as much as the content.

Automation amplifies what you put into it. If your base message is weak, sending it faster and at higher volume just annoys more people faster.

The approach: AI writes, automation sends

Separate the drafting problem from the sending problem. Use AI to write follow-ups that sound like you — specific, purposeful, appropriately brief. Then use an automation tool to handle the triggering and delivery at the right time.

You don't need a complex stack. The basics work:

  • AI drafting: ChatGPT, Copilot, or any LLM with a good prompt
  • Automation: Power Automate, Outlook rules, or Zapier — depending on what you're already using
  • CRM or spreadsheet: wherever you're tracking who needs what follow-up

Writing follow-ups that sound human

The key is giving AI enough context to write something specific. The less specific your prompt, the more generic the output. Here's a template I use in workshops:

Prompt Write a follow-up email from [Your name] to [Contact name] at [Company]. Context: We spoke on [date] about [topic/project]. They expressed interest in [specific thing] but said they needed time to [review / discuss internally / check budget]. Goal of this email: check in without pressure, remind them of the next step, and make it easy to reply. Tone: professional but warm. Length: 3–4 short sentences. No generic openers.

What you get back will feel like something a real person wrote — because the underlying intent is specific and genuine. The AI just helps you say it faster and more consistently.

Compare that to the alternative: "Write a follow-up email." That prompt produces the emails that get ignored.

Setting up a simple follow-up sequence

For outreach or collections scenarios, a three-touch sequence covers most situations:

  • Day 3 after no response: Short, direct. Reference the original message. Assume they missed it, not that they ignored it.
  • Day 10: Add a new angle — a relevant piece of information, a resource, or a question. Give them a reason to reply beyond the original ask.
  • Day 21: The graceful exit. "I'll assume timing isn't right — happy to reconnect if circumstances change." This message often gets the most responses.

Use AI to draft each of the three touchpoints with the context prompt above. Save them as templates. Then use Power Automate or your CRM's workflow engine to trigger them based on status changes or date conditions.

The one rule that makes automation feel human

Every automated message should contain at least one thing that couldn't have been sent to anyone else. A reference to the specific conversation you had. A detail about their situation. A question only relevant to their context.

That one personalisation signal does more work than any amount of friendly language. It tells the recipient: this was written for you, not queued for a list.

If your CRM captures notes from calls or meetings, feed those notes into your AI prompt. You'll get follow-ups that reference the actual conversation — and those get replied to.

Where to draw the line

Automation is a multiplier, not a replacement for judgment. Some situations should never get an automated response: a complaint, a relationship in crisis, a major decision point. Those get a personal message, written by a human, because the stakes are high enough that a generic follow-up would actively set you back.

Build that judgment into your process. Flag high-stakes contacts in your CRM and exclude them from automated sequences. The time you save on routine follow-ups goes back into the conversations that actually need you.

The short version: use AI to write follow-ups that are specific and genuinely useful, then use automation to make sure they go out reliably and on time. Done right, your contacts won't notice the automation — they'll just notice that you're consistently on top of things.
