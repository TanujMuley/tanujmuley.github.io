5 Prompts to Save 30 Minutes a Day

Tanuj Muley · May 2025 · 4 min read

Back to blog

Most people are underusing AI because they're waiting for the perfect use case. They want a dramatic transformation — a whole workflow automated overnight. In practice, the biggest gains come from small, boring improvements compounded daily. Fix how you write emails. Speed up how you summarise a meeting. That's 30 minutes a day. Five days a week. Over a year, that's more than three full workweeks back in your calendar.

Here are five prompts I use constantly, or teach in every workshop. Copy them, adapt them to your context, and use them today.

Prompt 1: Turn a messy thread into a clear summary

Email threads are information buried in noise. Instead of re-reading twelve replies to figure out where something landed, paste the whole thread into ChatGPT or Copilot and use this:

Prompt Summarise this email thread in plain English. Tell me: (1) what was decided, (2) what's still open, and (3) any action items with owners. Keep it under 150 words.

[Paste thread here]

The output is a crisp brief you can forward, reference in a meeting, or turn into a task. It takes about 20 seconds and replaces five minutes of re-reading.

Works best for: long project threads, heated back-and-forths that need a neutral summary, and catch-up emails after you've been out of office.

Prompt 2: Draft a reply without starting from scratch

Blank-page paralysis is real. Instead of staring at an empty compose window, describe what you want to say and let AI write the first draft. You'll edit it — but editing is much faster than writing.

Prompt Write a professional email reply to the message below. Tone: friendly but direct. I want to: [decline the request / agree but push the timeline / ask for more detail before committing]. Keep it to three short paragraphs max.

[Paste incoming email here]

Swap in your actual intent. The output is usually 80% ready — adjust the name, tweak the tone, and send.

Prompt 3: Turn raw meeting notes into an action log

After a meeting, you have a page of notes — or a transcript if you're using Teams or Zoom with AI enabled. Converting that into structured action items used to take 10–15 minutes. With this prompt:

Prompt From the notes below, extract: (1) all action items with the responsible person and deadline if mentioned, (2) decisions made, (3) topics to revisit next time. Format as a clean numbered list.

[Paste notes or transcript here]

The output goes straight into your project tracker or gets pasted into a follow-up email. Zero reprocessing required.

Prompt 4: Generate a first-draft report from bullet points

Most business reports follow a formula: what happened, why it matters, what's next. If you have the data, AI can write the narrative. You give it the facts; it gives you structured prose.

Prompt Write a one-page business update for a senior audience based on the data points below. Use three sections: Performance Summary, Key Highlights, and Next Steps. Be specific and avoid filler language. Assume the reader has 90 seconds.

[Paste your data / bullet points here]

This is particularly powerful for weekly or monthly reporting cycles. Once the format is set, updates become a 5-minute exercise instead of a 45-minute slog.

Prompt 5: Rewrite for clarity in one step

This is underrated. Before you send any important communication — proposal, escalation, performance note — paste it into AI and ask this:

Prompt Rewrite the following for clarity and conciseness. Remove jargon and filler. Keep the meaning and tone the same. Aim for 30% fewer words without losing substance.

[Paste your draft here]

You'll get a leaner version that says the same thing in half the space. Read both versions — you'll use the rewrite 95% of the time. The remaining 5% tells you something about why you wrote it the long way.

The underlying principle

Every one of these prompts follows the same structure: role, task, constraints, context. Tell the AI who it's writing for, what you want it to do, what the limits are, and what raw material it has to work with. The more specific you are, the less you edit.

Start with these five. Track how long each task used to take versus how long it takes now. After one week, you'll have your own list of the prompts worth keeping — and a clear picture of where the real time savings are hiding.
